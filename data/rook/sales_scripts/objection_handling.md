# Rook — Objection Handling Scripts

Each objection has: the exact wording to use, the underlying reason the
prospect is really saying it, and the pivot back to a next step.

---

## Objection 1: "Too expensive"

**What they really mean**: I'm not convinced the ROI justifies the cost, OR
I don't have that much cash right now.

### Response A — Anchor down to Entry tier (last resort)

> "I hear you. We do have a smaller package starting at $[entry price] —
> it's the lean version, gets you online with the essentials.
> Most clients end up moving up to [mid tier name] within a few months
> once they start seeing the traffic, but it's a solid starting point.
> Would that feel more doable?"

### Response B — Founding offer (if available)

> "Actually, if you're one of the next [N] to sign up, we're doing the
> full build completely free — building out our portfolio. Zero cost.
> You'd just leave us an honest review after. That changes the math entirely."

### Response C — Break down the ROI

> "Think about it this way — if the site brings in [2 / 3 / 5] new customers
> in the first month that you wouldn't have gotten otherwise, does that cover
> $[price]? For most [niche] businesses, one or two new clients pays for
> the whole site."

### Response D — The 50/50 payment reminder

> "And it's not all upfront — 50% to start, and the other 50% only when
> you're happy with the finished site. So you're only paying half today."

## Objection 2: "I already have a website"

**What they really mean**: I think my current site is fine, OR I'm lazy about
switching, OR I had a bad experience building/maintaining one.

### Response A — Probe the quality

> "How's it working for you? Is it pulling in new customers, or mostly
> just sitting there?"

*[If they say it's working great → acknowledge it and pivot to mockup as a comparison.]*
*[If they say it's mediocre → you have an opening.]*

### Response B — The mobile question

> "Is it mobile-friendly? Like, if someone's driving around looking for
> a [barber / restaurant / mechanic] and they pull up your site on their
> phone — does it look good and load fast?"

*[Most older sites fail this. If they're unsure → offer to show them the mockup.]*

### Response C — The Google question

> "When someone searches '[niche] in [city],' does your site show up on
> the first page? Are you beating your competitors there?"

*[If not → SEO angle. Their current site isn't working, it's just there.]*

### Response D — Show don't tell

> "Here's what I'll do — I already put together a quick mockup of what
> [Business Name]'s site could look like with a modern design. Takes
> 30 seconds to look at. Even if you don't do anything with it, it gives
> you a benchmark. Fair?"

## Objection 3: "Not interested"

**What they really mean**: I'm brushing you off because I assume this is
a generic sales pitch, OR I've been burned before, OR I genuinely don't see value.

### Response A — Separate from the pitch

> "That's fair — I get a lot of calls like this too.
> This one's a little different: I'm not trying to sell you anything on
> this call. I literally just want to show you what [Business Name]'s
> website could look like. I spent time building it for you already.
> Would it be weird if I just sent you the link?"

### Response B — Zero-risk offer

> "What if I just texted you the mockup link? You look at it whenever —
> or never. No follow-up if you don't want it. But you might be surprised."

### Response C — Respect the no, plant the seed

> "Got it, I'll leave you alone. Genuine question — is it 'not interested
> ever' or 'not interested right now'? No wrong answer, just helps me
> know whether to check back in a few months."

*[If "not right now" → set a 60-day follow-up. If "ever" → mark lost, move on.]*

## Objection 4: "I'm too busy"

**What they really mean**: This isn't a priority for me right now, OR I'm
overwhelmed and adding a website project sounds like work.

### Response A — Make it zero-effort

> "That's exactly why I do the whole thing for you. You don't build
> anything, you don't write anything, you don't figure out hosting or
> domains. You answer a few questions, I handle the rest.
> The only thing I need from you is 30 seconds to look at the mockup
> I already built."

### Response B — Respect the timing

> "When's a better time — next week? Next month? I'll put a note and
> call you then. What works better, beginning of week or end?"

### Response C — Email instead

> "No problem. Can I just shoot you an email with the mockup link?
> You look at it when you have 2 minutes. If it's not for you, no worries."

## Objection 5: "My nephew/son/friend handles that"

**What they really mean**: I feel loyal to someone I know, even if their
work isn't professional.

### Response A — Don't attack, ask a question

> "Oh nice — are they doing it for you as a favor, or do they do this
> professionally? I ask because sometimes it makes sense to keep it local
> and sometimes it doesn't — depends on what you need."

### Response B — The quality question

> "Is the site getting you new customers, or mainly just a place to point
> people? If it's working, great. If it's not doing much, might be worth
> a second opinion."

### Response C — Offer the mockup as a comparison

> "I hear you. Look — what if I just send over the mockup I built and you
> can show it to them? If they can match it, awesome, use them. If not,
> you have options. Sound fair?"

## Objection 6: "How do I know you're legit?"

**What they really mean**: I've been burned by web designers before, OR
I can't verify you're real.

### Response A — Point to the live site

> "Totally fair question. The portfolio is at curbsite.co — you can see
> sites we've built, read about the process, and see who's behind it.
> Steele, the founder, does a call with every new client himself.
> It's not a big faceless agency."

### Response B — Offer references

> "We've built sites for [RDND Photography / Yolonda's Place / other active
> clients — check CRM for approved references]. Happy to connect you with
> one of them if you want to hear firsthand how it went."

### Response C — The risk-free close

> "Here's why the risk is basically zero: you pay 50% upfront, and the
> second 50% only when you're happy with the final site. If you're not
> satisfied, you don't pay the rest. Simple as that."

---

## Nuclear Objection: "I'll think about it"

This is usually death. Convert to a micro-commitment before hanging up.

> "Absolutely, no rush. Can I ask — what would make this a yes?
> Is it price, timing, or just wanting to think it over?"

*[Let them answer. Then address the actual blocker, or set a specific follow-up.]*

> "How about this — I'll send you the mockup link and the full pricing breakdown
> in an email tonight. You look it over, and if it makes sense to talk more, 
> there's a link to book 15 minutes with Steele. No pressure, no follow-up call
> unless you reach out first."
